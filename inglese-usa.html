The United States: an inclusive growth model

US cities tend to focus less on physical overcrowding in historic centres and more on inclusive economic benefit. New Orleans is often cited for channelling tourism spending toward locally-owned businesses and music venues; Hawaii shows the opposite pressure - an economy so dependent on tourism that it has had to confront housing, water and reef impacts directly, sometimes through the same paid-reservation tools used at US national parks.

The US Constitution Compared to the Italian Constitution

The US is a federal, presidential republic: the President is elected through the Electoral College, not a direct national vote, and the Constitution (ratified 1788) is very difficult to amend - only 27 amendments in over two centuries. Italy, by contrast, is a parliamentary republic whose Constitution (1948) was born from the fall of Fascism and places strong emphasis on social and economic rights - exactly the vision championed by Giuseppe Saragat in his 1946 speech to the Constituent Assembly, and a much easier text to amend, through two parliamentary votes and, in some cases, a popular referendum.

Collegamento con le Conclusioni What both the Italian and American case studies show is that sustainable tourism succeeds only when it puts local communities at the centre - the same lesson explored, in Italian, in the final reflection. Vai alle conclusioni →

In sintesi

  • US cities focus more on inclusive economic benefit than on physical overcrowding: New Orleans channels tourism spending to local businesses, Hawaii confronts the costs of over-dependence on tourism.
  • The US President is elected through the Electoral College (538 electors), not a direct popular vote.
  • The US Constitution (1788) is very difficult to amend; the Italian Constitution (1948) is comparatively easier to revise and places far more emphasis on social rights.
